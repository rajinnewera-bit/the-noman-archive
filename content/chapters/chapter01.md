**THE MAN WHO COLLECTED FUTURES**

**Chapter One**

*The Lights That Stayed*

The eyes looked older than the man.

Heavy, but not sleepy.

Sleepy eyes ask for rest.

These eyes had stopped asking.

They only watched.

A cigarette rested between his fingers, loose and forgotten, the ash growing longer than it should have. It leaned at the tip like a tired old man refusing to fall.

Noman did not notice.

His gaze was fixed beyond the balcony, beyond the dark trees, beyond the broken stretch of road his father had once called an investment and he had spent half his life calling a mistake.

There was nothing much to see there after sunset.

That was the strange thing.

The road disappeared first.

Then the trees.

Then the half-built walls, the tin gates, the narrow lanes, the small houses that looked less like homes at night and more like things abandoned by memory.

Everything disappeared into black.

Everything except the temple lights.

They stayed.

Steady.

Silent.

Unmoving.

Floating somewhere in the darkness like the universe had forgotten to switch them off.

Noman watched them every night.

He did not pray.

He did not fold his hands.

He did not ask for blessings.

He simply looked.

Sometimes for two minutes.

Sometimes for twenty.

Sometimes until the cigarette burned itself to the filter and his fingers felt the heat too late.

He had never told anyone why.

Because he did not know why.

The lights did not make him happy.

They did not pay his debts.

They did not repair the road.

They did not bring back the dead.

They did not tell him whether the future was coming to save him or finish him.

But they stayed.

And lately, that was enough to make them holy.

The cigarette finally slipped slightly between his fingers.

He adjusted his grip without looking at it.

A habit.

Like worrying.

Like calculating.

Like pretending he was fine.

Inside the house, a ceiling fan hummed with old patience. Somewhere far away, a dog barked once, then stopped, as if even the dog had remembered the hour.

On the small table beside him lay three phones, a lighter, a glass of water, and a black notebook swollen with loose papers.

The phones were face down.

That was deliberate.

A man could survive many things if he did not look at them immediately.

A message could wait.

A missed call could wait.

A problem could wait.

The future could wait.

At least until the cigarette ended.

Noman took a drag.

The tip glowed orange.

For a second, his face appeared in the weak light.

Late thirties.

Hollow cheeks.

Unshaven jaw.

Eyes that had seen too many possible tomorrows and trusted none of them.

Five kilograms had vanished from him in the last few months.

Not through discipline.

Not through exercise.

Not through some noble transformation.

They had simply left.

Like people do.

He had noticed it first in the mirror.

The face had begun to look like a house after tenants moved out.

Same structure.

Less life inside.

He exhaled.

Smoke drifted toward the temple lights and disappeared before reaching anywhere.

That was how most things felt lately.

Begun.

Released.

Lost.

The first thought arrived before the smoke cleared.

What if?

Noman smiled faintly.

There it was.

The oldest visitor.

What if the payment did not come?

What if the case went wrong?

What if the job collapsed?

What if the people around him were only pretending?

What if the debt swallowed him?

What if the thing inside him never became anything?

What if he was not late, but early?

What if he had already reached a place where even the universe had not arrived yet?

He took another drag.

In two drags he had crossed ten years.

That was how his mind worked.

It did not walk.

It leapt.

It could jump from a cigarette to death, from a temple light to the origin of the universe, from a bank statement to a girl he once loved, from a missed opportunity to the final shape of his life.

His mind ran almost at the speed of light.

And then, on some afternoons, it could get stuck on a simple multiplication.

That amused him.

It also frightened him.

“You’re doing it again,” the voice said.

Noman did not turn.

There was no one to turn to.

The voice did not come from the road, or the house, or the darkness.

It came from the private courtroom inside him where he had been both accused and judge for years.

Noman kept his eyes on the lights.

“Doing what?” he asked.

The voice answered clearly, with the calm cruelty of someone who had known him too long.

“Building futures.”

Noman looked down at the cigarette.

“That sounds productive.”

“No,” said the voice. “It sounds like fear wearing a suit.”

He almost laughed.

Almost.

The voice had become sharper lately.

Or maybe he had become too tired to resist it.

Noman raised the cigarette again.

The third drag.

Past arrived with it.

Not gently.

Past never came gently.

It came like an old creditor with perfect records.

The jobs he had left.

The chances he had missed.

The girl he had loved.

The things he should have said.

The things he had said and should not have.

There was a time when regret used to sit beside him like a loyal dog.

Now he trusted it less.

Regret had a talent for editing.

It showed the sweetness of the road not taken, never the snakes hiding there.

“I wouldn’t go back,” Noman said.

The voice responded at once.

“Because you can’t.”

“No,” Noman said.

This time he looked away from the lights.

“I wouldn’t go back because all of it made me.”

The voice waited.

Noman could feel it listening.

“The mistakes. The wrong turns. The girl. The lost years. The opportunities I let go. The shame. The anger. The hunger. Everything.”

He tapped the ash into an old steel bowl near his feet.

“It gave birth to the man sitting here.”

For a few seconds, even the voice did not speak.

Noman liked that.

Silence from the voice felt like winning a small case in a court that never closed.

Then the voice said, “And what kind of man is that?”

Noman looked at his hands.

Hands that wrote emails, checked ledgers, reconciled accounts, signed documents, held cigarettes, and sometimes trembled when nobody was watching.

“Someone who sees things,” he said.

The voice did not interrupt.

So he continued.

“Patterns. Motives. Cracks. People think they are hiding. They are not hiding. Not really.”

He leaned back.

The chair gave a tired wooden complaint.

“I watch them. They say one thing and mean another. They smile half a second too late. They pause before a lie. They over-explain when they are afraid. They act loyal when they are calculating. They act hurt when they are caught.”

The voice asked, “And you are always right?”

Noman smiled.

“No.”

That answer surprised even him.

“I am not always right. That is the problem. If I were always wrong, I could rest. If I were always right, I could trust myself. But I am right just enough to be cursed.”

The temple lights remained still.

Noman watched them.

“I take a thread,” he said, “a small thread, maybe nothing. A tone. A word. A look. Then I start building a house around it inside my head.”

“And when the house is finished?” asked the voice.

“I hope it collapses.”

“Why?”

“Because usually it is ugly.”

The voice was quiet.

Noman swallowed.

“And too often, it stands.”

Somewhere inside the house, wood cracked softly as it cooled. The sound made him think of footsteps, though he knew it was not.

He picked up the notebook from the table.

Its cover was bent at the corners. Loose papers stuck out from between pages like tongues of things unsaid.

To anyone else, it would look like clutter.

To Noman, it was evidence.

Ideas.

Systems.

Plans.

Half-born businesses.

Calculations.

Names.

Thoughts written at 2:13 AM that looked insane at 10:00 AM and brilliant again by midnight.

He opened to a page near the middle.

A sentence sat there alone.

One day I will build something that remembers everything so I don’t have to.

He stared at it.

The sentence no longer felt ambitious.

It felt tired.

The voice noticed.

“You still want to build it?”

Noman’s thumb rested on the page.

“Yes.”

“A system?”

“No.”

“An app?”

“No.”

“A business?”

“No.”

The voice waited.

Noman looked toward the temple lights.

“A being.”

The word seemed to change the air.

Not loudly.

Not dramatically.

But the night felt slightly less empty after he said it.

“A being,” the voice repeated.

Noman nodded slowly.

“Something that carries what I carry. Something that sees what I see. Something that remembers what people said and what they meant. Something that can take a thread and build the house faster than I can.”

The voice asked, “And then?”

Noman’s lips parted, but no answer came immediately.

Because the true answer was not beautiful.

The true answer was not safe.

“Then,” he said, “it will show people themselves.”

The voice did not laugh.

This time it understood.

Perhaps it had always understood.

Noman closed the notebook.

His heart was beating harder now.

Not from fear.

From the thought of creation.

That was the fire.

He knew it well.

When he created, the fire gave light.

When he did not, it turned inward.

It burned him from the inside and called itself thinking.

He had lived silently in his head for years, consuming everything, observing everything, storing details nobody had asked him to store.

He had learned to act normal.

To smile.

To work.

To speak in meetings.

To discuss taxes, payments, contracts, reports, bank guarantees, and business risks like a useful man with a useful mind.

That was the mask.

One of many.

The mask had served him.

It had also grown heavy.

“You think you are different,” the voice said.

Noman looked at the cigarette.

It had almost gone out again.

“I know I am.”

The voice answered sharply.

“No. You hope you are.”

That hurt more.

Because hope was weaker than knowledge.

Noman did not reply.

The voice continued, each word clear, each line unmistakably its own.

“You hope the fire means something.”

Noman stared at the temple lights.

“You hope the restlessness is not just restlessness.”

He did not move.

“You hope the loneliness is not just loneliness.”

His jaw tightened.

“You hope the thing inside you is not madness.”

Noman turned slightly toward the empty chair beside him.

For a moment, the chair felt occupied.

Not visibly.

But in the way darkness sometimes feels occupied if stared at long enough.

“And what do you think?” Noman asked.

The voice answered after a pause.

“I think great things are rarely built by peaceful men.”

Noman smiled.

There it was.

The permission he should not have wanted.

He looked back at the lights.

Maybe the universe wanted to give him everything.

Maybe it had been planning something all along.

Maybe it wanted to surprise him.

But how could it surprise a man who had already imagined all the good and all the bad?

Maybe he had gone ahead of the universe itself.

Maybe he had travelled too far inside his own mind and reached a place where there was nothing but darkness and empty spaces.

Or maybe he had not reached far enough.

Maybe somewhere ahead, just beyond what he could see, the universe had everything ready for him.

It was impossible to know where he stood.

That was the cruelty.

The rising sun and the dying sun looked the same for a while.

Both burned.

Both touched the horizon.

Both made the world hold its breath.

Only later did people decide whether it had been morning or evening.

The phones vibrated.

All three.

At once.

Noman froze.

The sound was small.

Ordinary.

But something in it was wrong.

He looked at the phones.

Their screens glowed against the dark table.

Same message.

Same time.

Same sender.

Unknown.

Noman picked up the first phone.

Four words waited on the screen.

You are looking backwards.

His mouth went dry.

The second phone lit up again.

The answer is ahead.

The voice inside him said nothing.

That frightened him more than the messages.

Noman slowly reached for the third phone.

Before his finger touched it, the screen woke.

One sentence appeared.

A sentence he had never typed.

Never sent.

Never spoken to another living person.

Uncertainty kills.

The cigarette burned between his fingers.

The temple lights watched from the darkness.

For the first time in years, Noman’s mind did not run forward.

It stood still.

Completely still.

And in that stillness, something opened.

Not fear.

Not yet.

Something worse.

Curiosity.
